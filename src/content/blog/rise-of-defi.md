Decentralized finance — DeFi — refers to a collection of financial applications built on public blockchains that replicate and extend traditional financial services without relying on centralized intermediaries like banks, brokerages, or insurance companies.

## What Makes DeFi Different

Traditional finance requires trust in institutions. You deposit money in a bank trusting it will remain solvent. You use a brokerage trusting it will execute your orders honestly. DeFi replaces institutional trust with **cryptographic certainty** — smart contracts execute automatically according to their code, without human discretion.

The implications are significant. Anyone with an internet connection and a crypto wallet can access DeFi protocols. There are no accounts to open, no credit checks, no geographic restrictions, and no business hours.

## Core DeFi Primitives

### Automated Market Makers (AMMs)

Traditional exchanges use order books — lists of buyers and sellers whose orders are matched. AMMs like Uniswap replace order books with **liquidity pools**: smart contracts holding reserves of two tokens. Prices are determined algorithmically based on the ratio of reserves.

Liquidity providers deposit pairs of tokens into pools and earn a share of trading fees. Traders can swap tokens at any time against the pool, with price impact proportional to trade size relative to pool depth.

### Lending Protocols

Platforms like Aave and Compound allow users to supply assets and earn interest, or borrow against crypto collateral. All loans are **overcollateralized** — borrowers must deposit more value than they borrow — which eliminates credit risk and the need for identity verification.

Interest rates adjust algorithmically based on utilization: when most of the pool is borrowed out, rates rise to attract more supply.

### Yield Optimization

The composability of DeFi protocols — the ability to combine them like financial LEGO — enabled a category of automated yield optimizers like Yearn Finance, which automatically move capital between lending protocols and liquidity pools to maximize returns.

## Risks

DeFi's permissionless nature is also its primary risk vector. Smart contract bugs have led to hundreds of millions in protocol exploits. Liquidity crises, oracle manipulation, and governance attacks represent additional categories of risk that have no direct analogy in traditional finance.

Understanding these risks is not optional — it is the prerequisite for responsible DeFi participation.
